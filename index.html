Telekom Malaysia

My first job after graduating was as a Systems Analyst at Telekom Malaysia. I joined right in the middle of the internet boom around the year 2000 - a time when anything with the word ‘internet’ in it sounded like it was going to change the world overnight (and honestly, most of us believed it).

At that time, I was assigned to a new project to build a product called IOffice - short for Internet Office. The idea was to provide small and medium-sized businesses with internet-based services like email, telephone applications that worked through the data network, and a personal directory for storing phone numbers and email addresses, similar to a digital phone book. Back then, this felt like ‘the next big thing’.

As part of the team, I was trained to maintain the web servers, email servers, application servers, and Active Directory infrastructure. Everything was glued together using Java applications and a Java-based UI for the front end.

The systems were running on Sun Microsystems servers. At the time, Sun Microsystems was huge in the enterprise server world before the company eventually disappeared.

I also did some Java development work, and I even got the opportunity to travel to Los Angeles for training on maintaining the Sun server ecosystem. For someone early in their career, that was a pretty exciting experience.

Eventually, the IOffice project was discontinued because the product never really took off. After that, I worked in various areas within Telekom Malaysia until around 2005, when I joined a large SAP implementation project.

The company was replacing many of its legacy systems with SAP, and I became part of the team working on the Materials Management (MM) and Sales & Distribution (SD) modules.

I don’t know what SAP looks like nowadays, but back then it felt like working with a giant collection of predefined Excel-like forms running inside a proprietary application. Users navigated around the system using transaction codes, and underneath it all were thousands of database tables.

SAP came with ‘standard’ business processes for large organisations, but when those standard processes weren’t enough, companies customised the system using ABAP, SAP’s proprietary programming language.

I worked mainly on the functional side of the implementation. That meant working with business users to define requirements, mapping those requirements into SAP configurations, creating test scripts, performing system testing and UAT sessions, preparing training materials, and training users across the country.

That project gave me my first real exposure to large-scale enterprise system implementation.

The PhD Journey

After spending eight years working at Telekom Malaysia, I made the decision to return to university and pursue a PhD in Computer Science.

It turned out to be one of the most challenging journeys of my life. Studying for a doctoral degree is very different from studying for a bachelor’s or master’s degree. Instead of learning existing knowledge, you are expected to create new knowledge. You need to develop an idea that is both significant and original within your field, and your research is ultimately assessed based on those contributions.

During those years, I often questioned my decision. I had stepped away from the industry, given up a stable income, and put my professional career on hold to pursue another academic qualification. There were many moments when I wondered whether it was worth it.

Looking back, I am glad that I took that path.

The PhD gave me much more than a degree. It taught me how to think more critically, analyse problems more deeply, communicate ideas more clearly, and articulate my thoughts more effectively. These are skills that I continue to value and use to this day.

As part of my research, I published a paper and had the opportunity to present it at a highly respected conference in my field. It was a proud moment and one of the highlights of my doctoral journey.

Corporate Systems for Europe’s Largest Biomedical Research Institute

For this project, I was involved in building corporate systems for the newly established The Francis Crick Institute.

The corporate landscape consisted of multiple enterprise systems integrated together to support the organisation’s day-to-day operations. This was also my first experience working with Unit4 ERP, which at the time was still widely known as Agresso.

To me, it felt like a smaller and lighter version of SAP. The technology stack was still quite traditional, relying heavily on Windows servers, desktop applications, and native Windows tooling.

This was also where I learned a lot about Purchase-to-Pay (P2P) processes. Since the research institute relied heavily on scientific suppliers and procurement marketplaces for lab equipment and research materials, purchasing was a huge part of daily operations.

We worked in a relatively small team of fewer than 10 people, made up of business users, IT staff, and consultants. Together, we defined the business processes and configured the systems to support them.

I was involved throughout the full implementation lifecycle - from requirements gathering and system configuration to testing, UAT, training, and go-live support.

One of the bigger responsibilities I had was training around 200 researchers to use the P2P module during the go-live phase. Part of the support involved visiting researchers directly in their labs, including some biohazard-controlled environments, which was definitely different from normal office support work. It was actually quite interesting because I got to learn a little about the research they were working on while helping them navigate the system.

Building My Own Product

This was probably the most interesting chapter of my career because it was the first time I built something for my own company instead of for someone else.

Together with my co-founder, we built a marketplace for home bakers to sell their baked goods online.

Like every marketplace business, the biggest challenge at the start was the classic chicken-and-egg problem: you need sellers to attract buyers, but you also need buyers to convince sellers to join. It’s the kind of situation where everyone is politely waiting for someone else to make the first move.

I decided to start by focusing on getting sellers onto the platform first.

Convincing home bakers to join wasn’t easy. Many of them were unsure whether it was worth the effort or the risk. In some cases, I had to personally commit to buying their products upfront just to reduce that risk and give them enough confidence to list on the marketplace.

On top of building the system itself, I also had to think about marketing ideas, attracting buyers, onboarding sellers, and figuring out how to make the whole thing work operationally.

We spent years putting our own time, money, and energy into trying to make the business work. In the end, the marketplace never became profitable, and we eventually decided to shut it down.

I have no regrets about it.

It was a completely different kind of experience compared to working on projects inside established organisations. I got to experience the uncertainty, the risk, and the reality of trying to build something from scratch using my own resources - and not many people can honestly say they’ve done that.

Looking back now, I think the idea was what Y Combinator would call a tarpit idea - an idea that sounds promising on the surface but is extremely difficult to turn into a sustainable business.

Supporting an ERP System with a Gold-Level SLA

Later in my career, I had the opportunity to work as a support analyst for a university ERP system based on Unit4 ERP.

It was funny reconnecting with the same system around seven years later because, honestly, it still looked mostly the same. There were more web pages replacing some of the desktop screens, but for heavy financial operations, the desktop client was still the better option.

There were two incidents from that role that really tested me.

The first involved payroll.

A payroll superuser accidentally set a custom “U Balance” value to zero for all employees instead of for a single employee. The mistake affected nearly 400,000 employee records.

Luckily, the system had captured the original values before the update happened, and those values were printed into a text file.

At first, the obvious option seemed to be rolling back the system. But because there were so many integrations connected to it - including procurement and external marketplace integrations - rolling everything back would have created even bigger problems.

Instead, I decided to write a SQL recovery script to restore all the affected balances directly in the database using the values captured in the text file.

The script went through reviews and multiple rounds of testing before we finally deployed it into production.

That was probably one of the most stressful production incidents I’ve worked on, especially because it involved highly regulated payroll and financial data within an academic institution.

The second major challenge was upgrading the ERP platform from version 7.8 to 7.10.

I had never done that upgrade before, so I was heavily relying on documentation left behind by my predecessor. The upgrade process involved around 50 software packages spread across multiple servers.

The first attempt failed because of a mistake I couldn’t immediately identify.

At one point, I suggested that instead of spending endless hours trying to figure out what went wrong, we should just reset the environment and start over from scratch. Initially, people didn’t like that idea, but eventually everyone realised it would probably save more time.

The second attempt worked, and I completed the upgrade successfully on my own.

That experience taught me that sometimes restarting cleanly is faster and safer than endlessly debugging a broken state.

Discovery Project for Migrating a 20-Year-Old Finance System to SaaS

This was my first discovery project.

The organisation was using an old on-premise Unit4 ERP finance system, and the goal was to evaluate whether moving to a SaaS-based solution made sense.

I spent a lot of time interviewing users, learning how the business processes worked, understanding the issues they faced with the current setup, and researching what newer SaaS ERP platforms could offer.

What made the project interesting was that the deeper I looked, the more I realised the problems were not really caused by the system itself.

The bigger issues were around poorly designed account structures, inconsistent business processes, too many manual workarounds, and a lack of trust in the data.

Because the underlying data wasn’t reliable, people ended up creating additional manual processes outside the system just to produce accurate financial reports - which almost defeated the purpose of having a finance system in the first place.

A lot of stakeholders blamed the ‘clunky’ old ERP system, but during discussions I kept reminding them that even the most modern and expensive SaaS platform would not magically fix the problems if the same poor processes and bad data structures were carried over.

Data Migration with AI-Powered Automation

One of the most interesting projects I worked on involved building a new regulatory library using Microsoft Power Platform. The goal was to replace two long-running legacy systems that together stored nearly 30,000 regulatory documents and their associated processes.

What made this project particularly fascinating was that the legacy systems were not actually broken. They had been serving the business reliably for years. The real challenge was sustainability - the underlying technologies were aging, and there was growing concern that the platforms themselves would eventually become difficult to maintain or support.

My responsibility in the project was leading the migration of documents and metadata into the new platform.

Very early in the migration process, I encountered an unusual problem. One of the legacy systems did not store metadata in a structured database at all. Instead, metadata existed inside Word documents that acted like manually maintained index pages. These pages contained descriptive text and hyperlinks pointing to the actual regulatory documents. Over time, these Word documents had been converted into internal web pages accessible behind a login, effectively becoming a fully functioning document library without a traditional database underneath.

It was one of those moments where you realise how creative older systems could be. In today’s world of structured databases and APIs, it almost felt unbelievable that such a system had worked successfully for so long.

Because there was no structured metadata source available, the closest usable information came from the document filenames themselves. The filenames were not perfectly standardised, but they contained enough patterns to work with.

To solve this, I developed a Python automation script that used NLP techniques and regex-based extraction to identify and parse metadata from the filenames. The extracted data was then organised into Excel files for manual validation before being ingested into the new database alongside the regulatory documents themselves.

The second legacy system presented a different challenge. This system did have a database containing metadata for PDF documents, which made field mapping into the new platform more straightforward. However, one critical piece of metadata was missing: document titles.

Without document titles, navigating and locating documents in the new platform would have been much more difficult. To address this, I experimented with AI-based extraction using a BERT model. I trained the model to identify document titles from the first page of PDFs across multiple document formats and layouts. Using around 200 labelled samples, I iteratively trained and evaluated the model over several rounds until it achieved results that were accurate enough for practical use. A simplified version of my code for training the BERT model and using the fine-tuned model to extract titles from PDF documents is available on GitHub.

The model was not perfect, and AI rarely guarantees 100% accuracy in cases like this. However, it significantly reduced the amount of manual effort required and made the migration achievable within the available timeframe. More importantly, it enabled the new platform to retain meaningful search capabilities for thousands of migrated regulatory documents.

The project also gave me one of my first real opportunities to apply AI and LLM technologies to solve an actual business problem. Beyond simply using pre-trained models, I had the chance to fine-tune a model and experience firsthand how machine learning behaves in a real-world environment. It was a valuable experience that moved AI from being something theoretical into something practical and impactful.

Prototype built using Cursor

Prototype building with Cursor started as a side initiative alongside a project focused on enhancing existing systems with new features and functionality. Although my primary responsibility was not to build prototypes, I quickly realised they could play an important role in improving communication and requirement gathering.

My role was mainly centred around refining requirements and translating them into a backlog of JIRA tickets for the development team. Shortly after joining the project, I recognised that interactive prototypes would make the process significantly more effective. They allowed me to align with business stakeholders on two key aspects early on: how the screens should look and how the user journey and behaviours were expected to flow.

Since I already had a subscription to Cursor, which I use for personal software projects, I decided to experiment with building prototypes myself. Leveraging my previous software development experience, I was able to articulate requirements clearly and guide the AI effectively, which made the process surprisingly fast. A few years ago, producing the same level of prototype would likely have taken considerably more time and coordination.

I built the prototypes using Cursor and deployed the pages on Cloudflare for easy access. Interestingly, I later discovered that there was already a prototype kit available within the client organisation, although I had not been aware of it initially. After learning about it, I found that my approach using Cursor and Cloudflare was both quicker and easier to work with, while also making the prototypes more accessible for stakeholders and developers to review.

The prototypes became a valuable communication tool. They helped bridge the gap between business expectations and technical implementation, making conversations with both stakeholders and developers much clearer and more productive.

One of the key takeaways from this experience was recognising how effectively AI tools can accelerate day-to-day work when combined with strong domain knowledge and technical understanding. Rather than replacing expertise, tools like Cursor amplified my ability to communicate ideas quickly, validate requirements earlier, and move from concepts to tangible outcomes in a fraction of the time.
